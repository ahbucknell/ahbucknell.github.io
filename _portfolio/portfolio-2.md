DNA methylation is a key process within genomic defences to suppress the proliferation of transposons. This occurs through the RNA-directed DNA methylation (RdDM) pathway. RdDM uses 24 bp small RNAs to methylate CG, CHG, and CHH sites within the genome. However, plants within the Lemnoideae subfamily (duckweeds) lack typically conserved RdDM components and do not produce the 24 bp RNA. Despite this, duckweeds are capable of suppressing transposons through methylation suggesting the possibility of an alternative, non-canonical RdDM pathway.

Within the [Marí-Ordóñez](https://www.oeaw.ac.at/gmi/research/research-groups/arturo-mari-ordonez) group at the Gregor Mendel Institute, I focused on understanding induced methylation patterns of a transgene acting as a model for non-self DNA in *Lemna minor*. Alongside this was I completed an *in-silico* phylogenetics analysis of retrotransposons within three duckweed species.

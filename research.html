Protein design

Designing binders to probe effector function

Figure placeholder Predicted structure of a designed binder (rust) docked against an M. oryzae effector target.

Magnaporthe oryzae secretes a large repertoire of effector proteins during infection, many of which share little to no sequence similarity with anything of known function. Traditional reagents — antibodies, affinity tags, knockout lines — are slow to generate and often tell you very little about what an effector is actually doing inside the host cell.

I'm using novel, deep learning-based protein design to generate bespoke binding proteins against individual effector targets. Rather than screening existing libraries, these binders are designed directly against a target's predicted structure, which makes it possible to go after effectors that have no natural binding partner to start from.

The goal isn't just a better reagent. A binder that works tells you something about the surface it binds, and a designed binder that blocks a specific interface can be used to test whether that interface matters for virulence — turning a structural prediction into a testable hypothesis about mechanism.

Structural bioinformatics

Mapping the effector repertoire through structure

Figure placeholder Clustering of predicted effector structures by fold similarity across the M. oryzae secretome.

Effectors evolve fast. Sequence homology, the usual first tool for inferring function, mostly fails once you're comparing effectors across species or even within the same pathogen's expanded gene families. Structure is far more conserved than sequence, which makes it a better starting point for function prediction at scale.

Using high-throughput structural modelling, I generate predicted structures across the M. oryzae secretome and compare them against known folds and against each other. This doesn't give a definitive answer for any single effector, but it lets me group effectors into putative structural families, flag candidates that resemble known immune-evasion or enzymatic folds, and prioritise which targets are worth taking into the lab.

It's a triage step as much as a discovery one — a way of turning a list of hundreds of largely unannotated genes into a shorter list of testable hypotheses.

Transposon biology

Starships and the spread of virulence

Figure placeholder Schematic of Starship-mediated horizontal transfer of ToxA/ToxTA between fungal wheat pathogens.

Before moving into effector biology, my Master's research with Dr. Megan McDonald focused on Starships — giant transposons, found across Pezizomycotina fungi, that are capable of mobilising entire blocks of host genes between genomes3. We described Sanctuary, a Starship carrying the virulence factor ToxA, and showed it sits alongside an independently acquired copy of the related toxin ToxTA in fungal wheat pathogens1 — evidence that horizontal transfer of these elements is an active driver of virulence evolution, not just a historical curiosity.

That work has continued since: Tangerine, a Starship family identified in the lichen-forming fungus Xanthoria parietina, extended the known host range of these elements well beyond pathogenic fungi, into a symbiotic system with its own signatures of horizontal gene transfer between distantly related fungal partners2.

I keep half an eye on this question alongside my PhD work, because the same basic problem — how a fungal genome acquires and spreads a new virulence-associated gene — sits underneath both the Starship work and the effector biology of M. oryzae.

Introduction

Magnaporthe oryzae is a fungus that infects many different types of plants with blast disease. Hosts include, but are not limited to, many economically important crops such as rice, barley, millet, and wheat. It is considered one of the most devastating plant fungal pathogens, and is responsible for the loss of 30% of rice produced worldwide each year1.

During the process of infection, the fungus secretes specialised proteins into the plant cell. These proteins are called effectors, and promote the infection process through various means. The effectors can be detected by components of the plant intracellular immune system known as NLRs. After the effector is detected by the NLR, or a pair of NLRs, an effector-triggered immune response occurs. The consequence of this immune response can vary, but often involves killing the infected host plant cell and the invading fungus inside.

Understanding what these fungal effector proteins do, how they're recognised by NLRs, and by which NLRs (if any) are important questions to answer. Without answering these, generating novel resistance against blast disease is not achievable.

To answer these questions, we employ novel protein design techniques to generate completely new-to-nature proteins that bind to target effectors. While protein engineering has been a core technique within molecular plant sciences, it has been limited to modifying natural proteins. The ability to generate new-to-nature proteins unlocks new ways to explore effector function and is an advancement in current methods to broaden effector recognition by NLRs.

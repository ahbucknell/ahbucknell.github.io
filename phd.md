## PhD Research
By 2050 the global population is set to reach 10 billion. It is estimated that food production will need to increase by 50–70% to meet this demand. This will not only require higher crop yields, but also a reduction in losses from disease and pests. In rice alone, 30% of the global annual yield is lost to one disease – blast disease. The causative agent of blast disease is the fungal phytopathogen *Magnaporthe oryzae*. Like many plant pathogens, *M. oryzae* secretes small virulence proteins known as effectors into host cells. These proteins alter the host intracellular environment to promote pathogenesis and supress an immune response.

*M. oryzae* is predicted to carry 546 different effectors, known as *Magnaporthe* effector proteins (Meps). These effectors are known to be sequence diverse, but can show levels of conservation at the structural level. The advancement of protein folding algorithms such as AlphaFold2 and RoseTTAFold now allow for the high-throughput structural prediction of these Meps. My PhD research is focused on identifying structural homology between candidate MEPs and characterised proteins. Structural homology could indicate a similar function, allowing me to generate hypotheses of effector function based on characterisation of its structural homolog. These hypotheses can then be validated through traditional molecular and biochemical techniques.

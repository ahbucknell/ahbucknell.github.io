## PhD Research

This is made more difficult considering that 30% of global rice production, enough to feed 66 million people, is lost annually to blast disease. This disease affects numerous grass species including other agronomically significant crops such as wheat and barley.

The causative agent of blast disease is the fungal phytopathogen *Magnaporthe oryzae*. Like many plant pathogens, *M. oryzae* secretes small virulence proteins known as effectors into host cells. These proteins alter the host intracellular environment to promote pathogenesis and supress an immune response.

In total *M. oryzae* is predicted to carry 546 different effectors, known as *Magnaporthe* effector proteins (MEPs). These effectors are known to be sequence diverse, but can show levels of conservation at the structural level. The advancement of protein folding algorithms such as AlphaFold2 and RoseTTAFold now allow for the high-throughput structural prediction of these MEPs. My PhD research is focused on identifying structural homology between candidate MEPs and characterised proteins. Structural homology could indicate a similar function, allowing me to generate hypotheses of effector function based on characterisation of its structural homolog. These hypotheses can then be validated through traditional molecular and biochemical techniques.
